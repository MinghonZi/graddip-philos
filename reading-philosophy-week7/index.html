Epistemically unjustified

Argument 1

  1. If there is a successful cover-up, then both this will be difficult to detect and occasional anomalies will have heightened epistemic significance. \[\exists c (Sc \to (Dc \land \exists a (Oa \land Ea)))\]
  2. \[\exists c Sc\]

    3. Therefore,

    \[\exists a (Oa \land Ea)\]

Argument 2

  1. If some official sources of information have been co-opted to assist with covering up the truth, then all of them are not trustworthy. \[\exists s \exists c ((Os \land COOPT(s, c)) \to \forall s ( Os \to \lnot Ts))\]
  2. \[\exists s \exists c (Os \land COOPT(s, c))\]

    3. Therefore,

    \[\forall s ( Os \to \lnot Ts)\]

Epistemically unjustified

  • Selective cynicism

    • Conspiracy theorists have abnormally strong belief in $\exists c Sc$. They trust in their own capacities to perceive conspiracy.

  • Gullibility

    • $\exists a (Oa \land Ea)$ does not entail the anomalies are trustworthy. However, conspiracy theorists have exaggerated trust in them.

  • Prejudice

    • $\forall s ( Os \to \lnot Ts)$, but this seems to be a faulty generalisation. A better attitude could be: \[\exists s \exists c (Os \land (COOPT(s,c) \to \lnot Ts))\]

If those shoddy epistemic practices are corrected, then conspiracy theories could be considered as hypotheses.

Imposter syndrome

Argument 1 has the same structure

  1. If there is a successful cover-up, knowningly or unknowningly, then [...] \[\exists c (Sc \to (Dc \land \exists a (Oa \land Ea)))\]
  2. \[\exists c Sc\]

    3. Therefore,

    \[\exists a (Oa \land Ea)\]

Premise 2 is often justified.

Argument 2 is different

Argument 2

  1. Environmental reasons
  2. Psychological reasons

    3. Therefore,

  3. Certain external positive feedbacks are not trustworthy.

More rational ...

... but still seems unjustified

Similar justification

  • Selective cynicism

    • Trust in one's own capacities to perceive one's own inadequates.

    First Person Authority?

  • Gullibility

    • Exaggerated trust in external sources of negative feedbacks.

    Exploiting the premises of Argument 2, there might be a proof from heightened epistemic significance to trustworthiness.

  • Prejudice

    • Distrust in certain standard sources of information.

    Argument 2 justifies this.

In another essay, the author argues that the beliefs associated with impostor syndrome are often justified.

Believe vs Proof: Recipe for Disaster

Impostor Syndrome:

Not swayed by external validation like promotions + positive feedback – this just widens the gap between their perceived fraudulence and their achievements, thereby solidifying their imposter syndrome.

Conspiracy Theorist:

Not perturbed by the fact that superficial appears continue to reveal no conspiracy: this is an effective cover up.

Cross-Fertilisation of Study between Practices

P1: While there are differences with specific content, the pattern of belief formation and maintenance are epistemically nearly identical.

P2: Conspiracy is perceived to be socially more dangerous than imposter thinking (easily spread, undermines trust in democratic institutions).

P3: The default mainstream response to Critical Theorists is critical or dismissive, whilst to the impostor thinking it is more sympathetic.

Neutral vs Pejorative Definitions of Impostor Syndrome

“I have already suggested that the philosophical debate about neutral versus pejorative definitions of ‘conspiracy theory’ should have a counterpart in debate about the definition of ‘impostor syndrome’, and about whether sufferers from that syndrome are by definition thinking irrationally.” (Hawley, 2019)

Ameliorative Definition:

Imposter thinker’s beliefs have a specific content, and [their inadequacy in their position] is unjustified.

*Counterexample: Someone who is generally adequate in their job, but makes a mistake.

- Engages in ‘imposter thinking’ but not habitually
